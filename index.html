Introduction to Bacteriological Water Analysis

Foundations

Bacteriological water analysis is the scientific process of examining water samples to estimate the number (concentration) and type of bacteria present. It represents one critical aspect of overall water quality assessment.

Microbial quality is one of the primary indicators of drinking water safety. Bacteriological examination is performed routinely to ensure safe water for drinking, bathing, swimming, and domestic and industrial uses.

Why It Matters: Water acts as a vehicle for transmitting infectious diseases. Unlike chemical contaminants, microbial pathogens can be present at very low concentrations yet still cause serious illness — making systematic testing essential.

The Core Challenge

Testing water for microbial safety presents several fundamental difficulties:

🔢Low Numbers

Individual pathogen numbers may be too low to detect in a reasonably sized water sample, even when the water poses a real health risk.

⏱️Detection Time

Isolating and identifying some pathogens can take days, weeks, or even months — far too slow for routine safety monitoring.

🦠Diversity

Human faecal pathogens vary enormously in type — viruses, bacteria, protozoa — making it impossible to test each sample for every possible pathogen.

🚫No Certainty

The absence of one particular pathogen does not rule out the presence of another. Testing must be comprehensive.

The Solution: Instead of testing for every pathogen, microbiologists test for indicator organisms — non-pathogenic intestinal bacteria (mainly E. coli) whose presence signals faecal contamination and therefore probable danger.
🦠

Waterborne Microbial Pathogens

Pathogen Classification

Water can carry a broad range of pathogenic microorganisms. These are classified into seven main groups:

🦠 Bacteria 🔬 Viruses 🧫 Protozoa 🪱 Helminths 🌀 Spirochetes 🔴 Rickettsia 🌿 Algae

Bacteria

Disease Category Causative Organisms
Enteritis, Diarrhoea & Dysentery Campylobacter, Vibrio cholerae (Cholera), E. coli O157:H7, Salmonella, Shigella
Enteric Fever Salmonella typhi (Typhoid), Salmonella paratyphi (Paratyphoid)
Paralysis Clostridium botulinum (Botulism)
Eye, Ear & Skin Infections Miscellaneous bacteria
Urinary Tract Infections Escherichia coli, others

Viruses

🤢Gastrointestinal Disease

Rotavirus and Norwalk virus — major causes of waterborne diarrhoea and dysentery worldwide.

🫀Liver Damage

Hepatitis A and Hepatitis E — cause flu-like illness with significant liver damage; spread through contaminated water.

🦿Paralysis

Poliovirus — a waterborne enteric virus capable of causing permanent paralysis through contaminated drinking water.

Protozoa

Giardia

Causes giardiasis; resistant to chlorine at standard doses.

Cryptosporidia

Highly chlorine-resistant; a major threat in treated water supplies.

Amoeba

Can cause severe dysentery and, in some species, fatal brain infections.

Helminths (Parasitic Worms)

Worm eggs and larvae can survive in water sources. The main waterborne helminths include:

  • Round worm (Ascaris lumbricoides) — enters via ingestion of eggs
  • Tape worm (Taenia spp.) — ingested through contaminated food/water
  • Hook worm — larvae penetrate skin during water contact
  • Whip worm (Trichuris trichiura) — transmitted faecal-orally

Other Waterborne Pathogens & Water-Related Diseases

🌿Algae

Microcystis (cyanobacteria) produces powerful toxins. Dinoflagellates can produce shellfish poisoning toxins. Neither are bacteria but both contaminate water supplies.

🦟Water-Related Diseases

Transmitted by organisms that breed in water: Malaria (Anopheles mosquito), Dengue fever, Yellow fever, and Schistosomiasis.

Why We Test for E. coli & Coliforms

Indicator Logic
The Key Principle: Since it is impossible to test for every possible pathogen in every water sample, we instead test for harmless gut bacteria that always accompany faecal contamination. Their presence signals danger — even if we never detect the actual pathogen causing illness.

The Coliform Group — What Are They?

Coliforms are defined as:

  • Gram-negative (do not retain crystal violet stain)
  • Non-spore-forming rods
  • Aerobic to facultatively anaerobic (can grow with or without oxygen)
  • Ferment lactose to produce both acid and gas
🦠E. coli

The only true faecal coliform. Found exclusively in the intestinal tract of warm-blooded animals. Its presence in water is definitive proof of recent faecal contamination. Not normally found in fresh water or soil.

🌱Enterobacter aerogenes

Also a coliform, but widely distributed in nature — found in soil, on plants, and in the environment. Its presence alone does NOT indicate faecal contamination.

The Indicator Organism Principle

Indicator organisms are highly abundant gut bacteria used as proxies for faecal contamination. The reasoning is straightforward:

Why indicators work: Even when a person is infected with a pathogen, they excrete many millions of times more indicator organisms (like E. coli) than actual pathogens. Indicators are therefore far easier to detect, even when pathogen levels are below the threshold of detection.

Common indicator organisms include:

  • Escherichia coli — gold standard faecal indicator
  • Non-specific coliforms — broad screening
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa — commonly found in gut, indicates sewage
📊

The Four Indicator Organism Groups

Microbiological Indicators

Four groups of organisms are used as indicators of water quality, each with distinct characteristics, advantages, and laboratory detection methods.

Group 1

Total Coliforms

Total coliforms refers to a large group of Gram-negative, rod-shaped bacteria sharing several characteristics. The group includes thermotolerant coliforms, faecal bacteria, and some organisms from environmental (non-faecal) sources.

Important Limitation: The presence of total coliforms may or may not indicate faecal contamination. A high total coliform count with zero thermotolerant coliforms could be caused by soil entry or organic matter — not faecal contamination.
Growth temp: 35–37°C
Medium: Lactose-containing
Identification: Acid + gas production
Specificity: Low (non-specific)
Group 2

Thermotolerant (Faecal) Coliforms

These are coliforms that can grow and ferment lactose at the elevated temperature of 44–44.5°C. This higher temperature tolerance distinguishes true faecal organisms from environmental coliforms.

More than 95% of thermotolerant coliforms isolated from water are E. coli — making their presence near-definitive proof of faecal contamination. Confirmation of specific E. coli is therefore often unnecessary.

Caution: In nutrient-rich environments, some non-E. coli thermotolerant coliforms may grow. If an unexpectedly high count is found in seemingly clean water, seek specialist laboratory confirmation of E. coli.
Growth temp: 44–44.5°C
Medium: Lactose-containing
Identification: Acid + gas at 44°C
Specificity: High (faecal indicator)
95%+ are: E. coli
Group 3

Faecal Streptococci

These Gram-positive, chain-forming cocci include Streptococcus faecalis, S. faecium, and S. bovis. They possess Lancefield Group D antigens and grow in the presence of bile salts, sodium azide, and at 45°C.

Unique advantage: Faecal streptococci persist much longer in the environment and are highly resistant to drying. They may be detected even when thermotolerant coliforms are absent — useful when contamination occurred some time ago or far from the sampling point.

Note: Routine detection methods may produce false positives. Additional confirmatory tests (dye reduction using tetrazolium compounds or aesculin hydrolysis) are often required.
Gram stain: Positive
Morphology: Cocci in chains
Growth temp: 37–44°C
Medium: Sodium azide-containing
Antigen: Lancefield Group D
Detection: Tetrazolium reduction
Group 4

Sulphite-Reducing Clostridia & Clostridium perfringens

These are Gram-positive, anaerobic, spore-forming rods. The spores are extraordinarily resistant — they survive far longer in the environment than vegetative cells of other indicators and are much more resistant to chlorination.

Specialised use: Because of their long persistence, they are the best choice for water supplies sampled only occasionally or those with intermittent contamination (e.g. shallow wells, springs). Also used in marine sediment studies to detect the extent of sewage outfall contamination — detectable up to 10+ km from the source.

Gram stain: Positive
Growth: Anaerobic
Spore-forming: Yes
Chlorine resistance: Very high
Best for: Intermittent/distant pollution
Marine range: >10 km from outfall

Standard Water Analysis — The Three-Test System

Laboratory Protocol

Standard bacteriological water analysis uses a sequential three-test protocol. Each test builds on the previous one, progressively confirming the presence of coliforms with increasing certainty.

Overall Flow of Standard Water Analysis
Water Sample
→
Presumptive Test
→
Gas production?
No gas → Negative
   
Gas produced → Confirmed Test (EMB)
Only E. aerogenes → Negative
   
E. coli (green sheen) → Completed Test
Gram-negative rod + gas → ✅ Coliforms Confirmed
1
The Presumptive Test

Purpose: To screen for possible coliform presence by detecting lactose fermentation and gas production.

Principle

Water samples are inoculated into lactose broth tubes. Coliforms ferment lactose to produce acid and gas, which is captured in small inverted Durham tubes inside each broth tube. Gas production in any tube is presumptive evidence of coliforms — hence the name.

Procedure

1
Prepare nine tubes of double-strength lactose broth
Divide into three sets of three tubes.
2
Inoculate with three volumes
Add 10 ml to the first set, 1.0 ml to the second set, and 0.1 ml to the third set.
3
Incubate at 37°C for 24–48 hours
Read results at 24 hours. Re-incubate and read again at 48 hours if needed.
4
Count positive tubes and determine MPN
The Most Probable Number (MPN) of coliforms per 100 ml is estimated from the number of gas-positive tubes using statistical tables. More tubes = more sensitive test.
Limitation: Gas production alone doesn't confirm coliforms — other non-coliform bacteria can also produce gas from lactose. The test is only presumptive; further confirmation is needed.
2
The Confirmed Test

Purpose: To confirm that gas production in the presumptive test was indeed caused by coliforms, and to distinguish E. coli from E. aerogenes.

Medium: Eosin Methylene Blue (EMB) Agar

EMB agar is a selective and differential medium. The methylene blue dye inhibits Gram-positive organisms, allowing only Gram-negative coliforms to grow.

Reading Results

✅ E. coli (Positive)

Small colonies with a distinctive green metallic sheen. This is diagnostic for E. coli on EMB agar. Confirms faecal contamination.

⚠️ E. aerogenes (Negative)

Large, pinkish colonies without metallic sheen. Since E. aerogenes is naturally widespread in soil and environment, its presence alone does NOT indicate faecal contamination.

Interpretation Rule:
  • Only E. coli present → POSITIVE (faecal contamination confirmed)
  • Both E. coli and E. aerogenes present → POSITIVE
  • Only E. aerogenes present → NEGATIVE (no faecal contamination)
3
The Completed Test

Purpose: To definitively confirm that the organisms producing gas are Gram-negative, non-spore-forming rods — i.e., true coliforms.

Procedure

1
Subculture from EMB plate
Take organisms from the confirmed test EMB plate and use them to inoculate both a nutrient agar slant and a lactose broth tube.
2
Incubate at 37°C for 24 hours
Allow organisms to grow and ferment.
3
Check the lactose broth tube
Look for gas production — confirms lactose fermentation.
4
Perform Gram stain from nutrient agar slant
Examine microscopically to verify Gram-negative, non-spore-forming rod morphology.
Positive Completed Test: Organism is Gram-negative + non-spore-forming + rod-shaped + gas in lactose broth → Coliforms definitively confirmed in water sample.

Laboratory Methods for Coliform Analysis

Analytical Methods
Key Principle: Because analysis is always based on a small sample from a large water volume, all methods rely on statistical principles. Results are expressed as colony-forming units (cfu) per 100 ml or as a Most Probable Number (MPN).
Most Reliable Methods: The direct plate count (using VRBA agar) and the membrane filtration method (using mEndo agar) are considered most reliable.
1
Multiple Tube Method (Multiple Fermentation Tube Technique)

One of the oldest and most widely applicable methods. It uses serial dilution of the sample followed by incubation of multiple tubes at each dilution.

How It Works

1
Serial dilution
A 10 ml sub-sample is diluted with 100 ml sterile growth medium. Aliquots of 10 ml are then placed into each of ten tubes. The remaining is diluted again. After 5 dilutions, this produces 50 tubes covering dilutions from 1:10 to 1:10,000.
2
Standard procedure
The most common approach processes five aliquots from each of three consecutive 10-fold dilutions (e.g. 1:1, 1:10, 1:100). Each aliquot can be 1 ml into 10 ml single-strength medium, or 10 ml into 10 ml double-strength medium.
3
Incubation and reading
Incubate at a preset temperature for a set time. Coliforms are indicated by turbidity, a pH change, and/or the presence of gas (caught in inverted Durham tubes). Gas produced at 37°C strongly indicates E. coli.
4
MPN calculation
Count positive tubes at each dilution. Compare the pattern of positives to statistical MPN tables to estimate concentration. Result expressed as MPN per 100 ml.
Enhancement Tip: Using indicator medium (changes colour when acid is present) and Durham tubes (capture gas) makes results easier to read and interpret.
2
Membrane Filtration Method

The preferred method in most modern laboratories. Faster, more precise, and directly quantitative.

Principle

A measured volume of water is filtered under vacuum through a cellulose acetate membrane with a uniform pore diameter of 0.45 µm. Bacteria are retained on the membrane surface, which is then placed onto a selective culture medium and incubated. Characteristic colonies form and are counted directly.

Step-by-Step

1
Filter the sample
Pass the water under vacuum through a 0.45 µm membrane filter. All bacteria are retained on the surface.
2
Transfer membrane to growth medium
Place the membrane onto a suitable selective medium in a sterile container. Incubate at the appropriate temperature.
3
Count colonies
The membrane has a printed millimetre grid for counting colonies under a binocular microscope. Aim for 20–80 colonies per membrane (optimum counting range).
Advantages: Direct colony count (high precision). Results in ~18 hours. Less labour and glassware. Portable and field-adaptable.
Limitations: Cannot be used for turbid water (sediment, sludge clogs filter). Less sensitive than tube method. Consumables costly in some countries.

Results Expressed As

Each bacterium, clump, or particle with bacteria = one Colony Forming Unit (cfu).

Result reported as: Thermotolerant coliforms [Number] cfu per 100 ml

Turbid Water Handling: If the water quality is unknown, test two or more volumes. For very turbid samples, dilute in sterile diluent first. Samples can be split across two or more membranes, then counts added together.
3
ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) Testing

A rapid, modern method for detecting living microorganisms based on the universal energy molecule present in all living cells.

Principle

ATP is found only in and around living cells. Its detection gives a direct measure of biological concentration and microbial activity. ATP is detected by reacting with the enzyme firefly luciferase — the same reaction fireflies use to produce their bioluminescent glow.

The light emitted is proportional to the amount of ATP (and therefore living microorganisms) present. A luminometer measures the light output precisely.

💡The Luciferase Reaction

ATP + Luciferin + O₂ → Light (bioluminescence)
More ATP → More light → More living bacteria

The amount of light produced is directly proportional to the amount of biological energy (ATP) in the sample.

Second Generation ATP Tests are specifically designed for water, wastewater, and industrial applications where sample components could interfere with the basic ATP assay. They use purified reagents to eliminate background interference.
4
Plate Count Method

A direct counting method in which bacteria grow into visible colonies on solid culture medium. Widely used for evaluating the effectiveness of water treatment.

Optimal Colony Range

Too few (<30 colonies): Statistically unreliable — cannot draw valid conclusions from too few data points.
Too many (>300 colonies): Colonies overlap and merge, making accurate counting impossible.
Target range: 30–300 colonies per plate — statistically valid and practically countable.

Procedure

1
Serial dilutions
Make dilutions of 1:10, 1:100, 1:1000, etc. in sterile water to hit the optimal colony range.
2
Plate on nutrient agar
Culture dilutions on plate count agar (general) or MacConkey agar (Gram-negative, e.g. E. coli specifically).
3
Dual-temperature incubation
One set of plates at 22°C for 24 hours (environmental organisms); a second set at 37°C for 24 hours (faecal/gut organisms).
4
Count colonies by eye
Colonies are typically a few mm across — no microscope needed. Some modern methods use fluorescent agents to allow automated counting.
5
Pour Plate Method

A variant of the plate count method specifically designed for anaerobic or microaerophilic bacteria that grow poorly in air.

Principle

Serial dilutions of the sample are mixed into liquid (molten) nutrient agar. The mixture is poured into bottles which are sealed and laid on their sides to produce a sloping agar surface. This creates semi-anaerobic conditions inside the agar body. Colonies that develop throughout the medium (not just on the surface) are counted after incubation.

Key Measurement: Total Viable Count (TVC)

Total Viable Count (TVC) = total number of living bacteria in the sample.

Unit: cfu/ml (colony forming units per millilitre)

Formula: TVC = Colony count × Dilution factor

⚖️

Comparison: Multiple Tube vs Membrane Filtration

Method Comparison

These two methods are the most commonly used in routine water testing. Each has distinct advantages and limitations that determine when to use each.

Feature Multiple Tube (MFT) Membrane Filtration
Speed Slower — 48 hrs for confirmed positive Faster — results in ~18 hours
Labour More labour-intensive Less labour-intensive
Precision Indirect / statistical (lower precision) Direct colony count (higher precision)
Sensitivity More sensitive Less sensitive
Water Types Applicable to ALL water types NOT suitable for turbid water
Field Use Not readily field-adaptable Readily adapted for field use
Glassware Requires more glassware Requires less glassware
Culture Medium Requires more medium Requires less medium
Consumables Widely available, lower cost Higher cost, less available
Result Type MPN (estimated) cfu/100 ml (direct)
When to Choose Which:
Use Membrane Filtration for clear, treated water where speed and precision matter.
Use Multiple Tube for turbid, highly contaminated, or difficult samples, or when working in low-resource settings.

Culture Media Used in Water Bacteriology

Laboratory Media

Selecting the right culture medium is critical — each medium is designed to either encourage specific organisms (selective), reveal specific characteristics through colour change (differential), or both.

1. MacConkey Agar

Purpose: Selective and differential medium for Gram-negative bacteria, primarily for detecting lactose fermenters like E. coli.

Key components and their roles:

  • Bile salts — inhibit most Gram-positive bacteria
  • Crystal violet dye — additional inhibition of Gram-positive organisms
  • Neutral red dye — stains lactose-fermenting colonies red/pink
  • Lactose — fermented by target organisms; fermenters become coloured, non-fermenters remain colourless
  • Peptone — nitrogen and carbon source for growth

2. Endo Agar (mEndo Agar)

Purpose: Originally developed for isolating Salmonella typhi; now standard in membrane filtration for total coliform detection.

Key components: Peptone, lactose, dipotassium phosphate, agar, sodium sulfite, basic fuchsin.

Results:

  • Coliform organisms ferment lactose → colonies turn red
  • Non-lactose-fermenting organisms → clear, colourless colonies against a faint pink background

3. mFC (Membrane Faecal Coliform) Medium

Purpose: Specifically used in membrane filtration for detecting faecal coliforms. Contains both selective and differential agents.

Key components and roles:

  • Rosolic acid — inhibits most bacteria except faecal coliforms
  • Bile salts — inhibit non-enteric bacteria
  • Aniline blue — acts as a pH indicator; faecal coliforms fermenting lactose produce acid, causing a colour change

4. VRBA (Violet Red Bile Agar)

Purpose: Used in the direct plate count method for coliform detection. Contains bile salts that promote growth of Gram-negative bacteria while partially inhibiting Gram-positive organisms (though not completely).

Contains lactose — lactose-fermenting bacteria produce coloured colonies; non-fermenters produce colourless ones.

5. TYEA (Tryptone Yeast Extract Agar)

Purpose: A non-selective general-purpose medium used to determine the overall level of contamination (also known as a colony count / standard plate count).

Composition: Tryptone, yeast extract, common salt, L-arabinose in glass distilled water.

Incubation: Cultivated at two temperatures simultaneously — 22°C (environmental organisms) and 36°C (gut/faecal organisms) — to capture a full spectrum of bacterial contamination.

6. EMB (Eosin Methylene Blue) Agar

Purpose: Selective and differential medium used in the Confirmed Test of standard water analysis.

Mechanism: Methylene blue inhibits Gram-positive organisms. Coliforms that ferment lactose produce dark-centered colonies. E. coli specifically produces a characteristic green metallic sheen — a hallmark diagnostic feature.

Lactose Fermentation — The Core Principle Behind All These Media: Most selective media for coliform detection rely on the fact that coliforms ferment lactose to produce acid and sometimes gas. Coloured indicators (neutral red, aniline blue, basic fuchsin) change colour in response to this acid production, making coliform colonies visually distinct from non-coliform colonies.

Key Terms Glossary

Reference
Coliform

Gram-negative, non-spore-forming, facultatively anaerobic rod that ferments lactose to acid and gas. Used as an indicator of faecal contamination.

MPN (Most Probable Number)

A statistical estimate of the number of viable organisms per unit volume, derived from the number of positive tubes in a multiple tube test.

cfu (Colony Forming Unit)

One bacterium, clump of bacteria, or particle with bacteria that gives rise to a single visible colony on culture medium. The unit of measurement in plate-based methods.

Thermotolerant Coliform

A coliform that grows and ferments lactose at 44–44.5°C. Strongly indicates faecal origin. Over 95% are E. coli.

Durham Tube

A small inverted tube placed inside a broth tube. Captures gas produced during fermentation, providing a visible indicator of positive growth.

Indicator Organism

A harmless microorganism whose presence signals likely contamination with pathogenic organisms. Present in far greater numbers than pathogens, making them easier to detect.

TVC (Total Viable Count)

The total number of live bacteria detectable in a sample, measured by the pour plate method. Expressed as cfu/ml.

Selective Medium

A culture medium containing agents that inhibit certain organisms while permitting target organisms to grow.

Differential Medium

A culture medium that produces visually distinct colonies (e.g. different colours) for different organisms, allowing identification without further tests.

Luciferase

The enzyme used in ATP testing to produce bioluminescence. Catalyses the reaction between ATP and luciferin, producing light proportional to the amount of ATP present.

EMB Agar

Eosin Methylene Blue agar — used in the Confirmed Test. Inhibits Gram-positives; produces a green metallic sheen on E. coli colonies.

Faecal Streptococci

Gram-positive chain-forming cocci (e.g. S. faecalis) used as indicators of faecal contamination. More environmentally persistent than coliforms.

Revision Summary

Study Essentials
🔑 The Big Picture

Bacteriological water analysis cannot test for every possible pathogen. Instead, it uses indicator organisms — primarily E. coli — as proof of faecal contamination. Where there is faecal contamination, dangerous pathogens may also be present.

🦠 The 4 Indicator Groups to Know
  1. Total Coliforms — broad screen; may not mean faecal origin
  2. Thermotolerant Coliforms — grow at 44°C; near-definitive faecal indicator; 95% are E. coli
  3. Faecal Streptococci — persist longer; use when contamination is old or distant
  4. Sulphite-reducing Clostridia — spore-forming; chlorine-resistant; best for intermittent/marine contamination
🧪 The 3-Stage Water Test Sequence
  1. Presumptive Test → lactose broth + Durham tube → gas = possible coliforms → MPN estimate
  2. Confirmed Test → EMB agar → green sheen (E. coli) = positive; large pink only (E. aerogenes) = negative
  3. Completed Test → Gram stain + gas from lactose = definitively confirmed coliform
🔬 The 5 Methods — Key Identifiers
  1. Multiple Tube — oldest; MPN by statistics; works with all water types; slower
  2. Membrane Filtration — fastest; direct cfu count; fails with turbid water
  3. ATP Testing — detects living cells by bioluminescence (luciferase); most rapid
  4. Plate Count — 30–300 colonies target; dual incubation (22°C + 37°C)
  5. Pour Plate — for anaerobes; colonies in agar body; gives TVC in cfu/ml
🍽️ Media — Quick Reference
  • MacConkey Agar — Gram-negative selection; lactose fermenters = pink/red colonies
  • Endo Agar / mEndo — membrane filtration; coliforms = red; non-fermenters = colourless
  • mFC Medium — faecal coliforms only; rosolic acid + bile salts + aniline blue indicator
  • VRBA — direct plate count method; bile salts inhibit Gram-positive
  • TYEA — non-selective general count at 22°C and 36°C
  • EMB Agar — confirmed test; E. coli = green metallic sheen; E. aerogenes = large pink
