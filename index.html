Unit 1 Food Microbiology

What is Food Microbiology?

Food microbiology is the scientific study of microorganisms that live in, create, or contaminate food. Understanding these organisms is critical for food safety, production, and preservation.

ℹ️
Core DefinitionFood microbiology covers microorganisms that cause spoilage, pathogens that cause disease if food is improperly handled, and organisms used to produce fermented foods like cheese, yogurt, bread, beer, and wine.

The Dual Nature of Microorganisms in Food

Microorganisms play two opposing roles in food — they can be either incredibly useful or dangerously harmful:

✅ Beneficial Roles

  • Production of cheese, yogurt, and sausages
  • Fermentation of beer, wine, and bread
  • Pickling and sauerkraut preparation
  • Probiotics that improve gut health

❌ Harmful Roles

  • Causing food spoilage (bad smell, taste, appearance)
  • Producing dangerous toxins in food
  • Causing serious and fatal food poisoning
  • Contaminating raw and processed foods

Why Study Food Microbiology?

🛡️ Prevent Spoilage

Understand how and why food decays so it can be prevented through proper storage and handling.

🏥 Prevent Illness

Identify pathogenic microorganisms that contaminate food and cause disease in humans.

🏭 Production

Harness beneficial microbes for food production, preservation, and improving nutritional value.

Unit 1 Beneficial Microorganisms

Probiotics & Beneficial Microbes

Not all microorganisms are harmful. Many are essential to human health and the production of common foods.

✅
Probiotics DefinedProbiotics are living microorganisms that, when consumed in adequate amounts, confer health benefits beyond their basic nutritional value. There is growing evidence for their role in gastrointestinal infections, irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), and inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).

Lactobacillus Species

Lactobacillus species are among the most widely used beneficial bacteria. They are used in the production of yogurt, cheese, beer, wine, cider, and chocolate. In recent years, there has been great interest in their use as probiotics for disease prevention in both humans and animals.

Bifidobacteria

Bifidobacteria are considered highly important probiotics used in the food industry to relieve and treat many intestinal disorders. They exert a wide range of beneficial health effects:

  1. Regulation of intestinal microbial homeostasis — maintaining a healthy balance of gut bacteria.
  2. Inhibition of harmful pathogens — preventing harmful bacteria from colonising or infecting the gut lining.
  3. Modulation of immune responses — influencing both local (gut) and systemic (whole-body) immune activity.
  4. Suppression of cancer-promoting enzymes — reducing procarcinogenic enzymatic activity within the gut microbiota.
  5. Production of vitamins — synthesising important vitamins directly in the gut.
  6. Bioconversion of dietary compounds — converting dietary substances into bioactive, health-promoting molecules.

Microbial Biopolymers in Food

Microorganisms naturally produce a variety of biopolymers — large biological molecules — including polysaccharides, polyesters, and polyamides. Several of these microbially produced polymers are used as functional ingredients and additives in the food industry.

Unit 1 Food Spoilage

Understanding Food Spoilage

Food spoilage is damage or deterioration that makes food unsuitable for human consumption. It is not always visible — food can look perfectly normal yet be dangerously contaminated.

⚠️
Important NoteFood must be considered spoiled if it is contaminated with pathogenic microorganisms or poisonous agents such as pesticides or heavy metals — even if it looks, smells, and tastes perfectly fine. Always verify through proper bacteriological and toxicological testing.

Causes of Food Spoilage

a) Microbial Activity

Bacteria, yeasts, and molds produce enzymes that decompose food components. Bacteria are the most significant spoilage agents in high-moisture foods like milk and meat. Molds are the primary spoilage agents in low-moisture foods like dry cereals.

b) Enzyme Activity (Autolysis)

After the death of a plant or animal, enzymes naturally present in tissues continue working, breaking down food components in a process called autolysis. This is why even clean, uncontaminated meat will deteriorate over time.

c) Chemical Reactions

Non-enzymatic chemical reactions also cause spoilage. The most common example is oxidative rancidity — the oxidation of fats in food — which produces unpleasant off-flavours without any microbial involvement.

d) Vermin

Insects (weevils, ants, cockroaches, fly larvae), rodents (rats, mice), and birds cause spoilage through physical consumption of food, contamination with pathogens they carry, and the aesthetic damage their presence causes.

e) Physical Changes

Freezing, burning, drying, mechanical pressure, and other physical forces can alter food structure and quality, rendering it unacceptable even without microbial involvement.

Unit 1 Contamination

Sources of Microorganisms in Food

Microorganisms can enter food from a wide variety of environmental and biological sources throughout growing, processing, and handling.

🌱 Soil & Water

Primary reservoir of bacteria, molds, and yeasts. Irrigation water and soil contact introduce organisms to crops.

🌿 Plants & Plant Products

Fruits, vegetables, and grains carry surface microorganisms from growing environments.

🍽️ Food Utensils

Improperly cleaned cutting boards, knives, and processing equipment transfer microbes between foods.

🦠 Intestinal Tract

Intestines of humans and animals harbour pathogens like Salmonella and E. coli that can contaminate meat during slaughter.

👤 Food Handlers

Workers with poor hygiene, skin infections, or illness are a major contamination source, especially for Staphylococcus aureus.

🐄 Animal Hides & Skins

External surfaces of animals carry environmental microorganisms that can contaminate meat during processing.

💨
Air & DustAirborne particles and dust carry mold spores and bacteria, which can settle on food during processing, packaging, or storage — particularly relevant in bakeries and open food facilities.
Unit 1 Growth Factors

Intrinsic Factors Affecting Microbial Growth

Intrinsic factors are properties that are inherent to the food itself. They determine how hospitable a food environment is for microbial growth.

1. pH (Acidity/Alkalinity)

Most bacteria thrive at neutral to slightly alkaline pH (6.8–7.5). However, different organisms have different pH tolerances:

pH Tolerance Ranges

  • Most bacteria: pH 4.0 – 9.0
  • Salmonella: pH 4.5 – 8.8
  • E. coli: pH 4.4 – 9.0
  • Molds: pH 1.5 – 11.0 (widest range)
  • Yeasts: pH 1.5 – 8.5

pH of Common Foods

  • Beef: 5.1 – 6.2
  • Chicken: 6.2 – 6.4
  • Milk: 6.3 – 6.8
  • Cheese: 4.9 – 5.9
  • Fruits: mostly below 3.5
  • Vegetables: 3.0 – 6.1
🔬
Acidophilic OrganismsMicroorganisms that can grow in highly acidic environments (around pH 2.0) are called acidophilic. Yeasts and molds generally fall into this category. In contrast, Vibrio cholerae is acid-sensitive and prefers alkaline conditions. Most bacteria are killed in strongly acidic or alkaline environments — with the notable exception of Mycobacteria, which are unusually acid-resistant.

2. Water Activity (Moisture Content)

The relevant property isn't just the amount of water, but the water activity (Aw) — a measure of the free, available water in food. It is defined as the ratio of the vapour pressure of the food to that of pure water at the same temperature:

Aw = VPFood / VPWater

Pure water has an Aw of 1.0. All food products have Aw values less than 1.0. When Aw drops below a microorganism's minimum requirement, growth stops entirely.

MicroorganismMinimum Aw for Growth
Clostridium botulinum, Bacillus cereus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Salmonella spp.0.95
Staphylococcus aureus (anaerobic), Candida spp., Saccharomyces0.90

3. Nutrient Content

Microorganisms require proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, water, energy, nitrogen, sulfur, phosphorus, vitamins, and minerals for growth. Foods rich in these nutrients — such as milk, meat, and eggs — are highly susceptible to microbial spoilage because they provide an ideal growth medium.

4. Naturally Occurring Antimicrobial Substances

Many foods contain built-in chemical defences against microbial attack. Examples include:

  • Lactenin & anti-coliform factors — naturally present in fresh milk
  • Lysozyme — an enzyme in egg white that destroys bacterial cell walls

5. Biological Structures

Physical structures in food act as barriers to microbial entry:

  • Meat: fascia (connective tissue), skin, and membranes prevent microbes from penetrating deeper tissue
  • Eggs: the shell and inner membranes protect the yolk and albumen from infection
  • Fruits: peel and skin provide a physical barrier
Unit 1 Growth Factors

Extrinsic Factors Affecting Microbial Growth

Extrinsic factors are conditions external to the food that determine whether microorganisms can grow and thrive in a given environment.

1. Temperature

Temperature is one of the most important determinants of microbial growth. Bacteria are classified into three groups based on their optimal temperature range:

❄️ Psychrophilic

Growth: −10°C to 20°C
Optimum: ~20°C

Found in soil and water. Can cause food spoilage even in cold storage. Many belong to the Pseudomonas genus.

🌡️ Mesophilic

Growth: 5°C to 45°C
Optimum: ~37°C

Most pathogens are mesophilic. Grow best at body temperature. None can grow below 5°C or above 45°C.

🔥 Thermophilic

Growth: Above 45°C
Optimum: 50°C–70°C

Mainly spore-formers. Significant in processed and canned foods where high temperatures are applied.

💡
Temperature InteractionsThe effect of temperature on microbial growth is not independent — it also depends on the pH of the food, the water activity, and the availability of growth factors in the nutrient medium. A combination of unfavourable conditions provides greater protection than any single factor alone.

2. Gaseous Environment

The presence and concentration of oxygen in the food environment determines which microorganisms can grow:

🌬️ Aerobes

Require high oxygen. Grow on the surface of raw meat and in insufficiently dried or salted products (especially molds).

🚫 Anaerobes

Grow without oxygen. Thrive in canned foods — most dangerous is Clostridium botulinum producing botulism toxin.

🔄 Facultative Anaerobes

Can grow in either the presence or absence of oxygen. Examples include Salmonella and E. coli — very versatile and dangerous.

📦 Microaerophiles

Require low oxygen. Most likely to grow in vacuum-packed foods. Campylobacter jejuni is a key example.

3. Relative Humidity

Relative humidity refers to the amount of moisture in the storage atmosphere. Foods with low water activity placed in a high-humidity environment will absorb moisture from the air, raising their Aw to levels that support microbial growth. A classic example: dry grains stored in humid conditions will take up water and subsequently undergo mold spoilage.

Unit 1 Preservation

Food Preservation Methods

Food preservation uses physical and/or chemical agents to prevent microbial spoilage and extend the storage life of food. Two fundamental principles guide all preservation strategies.

Principle 1: Inhibition

Growth and multiplication of microorganisms is slowed or stopped, but organisms may not be killed. If the inhibiting influence is removed, spoilage can resume.

  • Reducing water activity (drying, salting)
  • Reducing pH (fermentation, adding acids)
  • Using chemical preservatives (e.g. sodium benzoate)
  • Low temperature storage (chilling or freezing)
  • Smoking (combined drying and preservative effect)

Principle 2: Killing

Spoilage microorganisms are destroyed, and the food is then protected against recontamination by sealing in airtight containers.

  • Heat treatment (pasteurisation or sterilisation)
  • Irradiation (gamma rays, cobalt-60) — destroys DNA
  • Gas treatment (ethylene oxide or ozone) — kills vegetative cells and spores

Preservation by Lowering pH

Reducing pH to below 4.5 prevents growth of most spoilage and pathogenic bacteria. This is achieved by:

  • Fermentation: microorganisms break down carbohydrates anaerobically into alcohol or lactic acid and CO₂, naturally acidifying the food.
  • Addition of acids: direct addition of acetic acid (vinegar) or citric acid lowers pH rapidly.

Preservation by Lowering Water Activity

The Aw of a food can be reduced through these methods:

🧂 Salting

Sodium chloride (and sometimes nitrates/nitrites) draws water out of food through osmosis. Dry cure, brine pickling, injection cure, or direct addition methods are used.

🍬 Adding Sugar

Monosaccharides (glucose, fructose) are more effective than disaccharides like sucrose. Note: osmophilic yeasts can tolerate very high sugar concentrations and may still cause spoilage.

☀️ Drying

Sun/air drying, electrical (convection) drying, or freeze-drying all reduce available water to levels that prevent microbial growth.

Preservation by Low Temperature

Chilling (Cold Storage)

Keeping food between 0°C and 15°C (most commonly at 4–5°C). Slows microbial growth but does not stop it entirely. Spores are unaffected by chilling.

Freezing

Keeping food at 0°C to −35°C. Stops most microbial growth completely. Spores are not killed by freezing. However, most parasites are killed by freezing.

Heat Treatment — Key Terminology

🔥
D-Value (Decimal Reduction Time)The time required at a given temperature to destroy 90% of the microorganism population. The higher the temperature, the shorter the D-value. Example: Clostridium sporogenes at 120°C has a D-value of 1 minute; at 110°C it is 10 minutes.
🌡️
Z-ValueThe number of degrees Celsius the temperature must be raised to reduce the thermal death time by a factor of 10. For bacterial spores, the Z-value is typically 10°C. Example: if 1 min at 120°C kills 90% of spores, then 10 min at 110°C achieves the same effect.
⏱️
F-ValueExpresses the total heat treatment applied to food, equivalent to minutes at 121°C. F = 1 means 1 minute at 121°C. The same F-value can be achieved at lower temperatures with longer times: F=2 at 121°C = 20 minutes at 111°C.

Unit 1 Fermentation

Fermented Foods

Fermentation is the breakdown of carbohydrates under anaerobic conditions into alcohol or lactic acid and carbon dioxide. It is one of humanity's oldest food preservation methods and produces some of the world's most loved foods and beverages.

🍺 Beer Production

Beer is produced by the fermentation of malted grain. Malting involves germinating grain (barley, rye, or wheat) and then drying it in a kiln, which activates α- and β-amylase enzymes needed for fermentation.

1
Mashing

Malted grain (grist) is mixed with heated water. A series of temperature rests activates different enzymes: proteinases (49–55°C), β-gluconase (60°C), and amylases that convert starch to fermentable sugars like maltose (65–71°C).

2
Wort Preparation

Spent grain is separated. The liquid (wort) is boiled in a large kettle with hops and other flavourings, then spun in a whirlpool to clarify.

3
Fermentation

Yeast is added ("pitched") to the fermentation tank. Top-fermenting yeasts produce ales; bottom-fermenting yeasts produce lagers. Fermentation takes 1–3 weeks to produce "green beer."

4
Conditioning & Packaging

Green beer is transferred to conditioning tanks where yeast settles and beer is carbonated. Most beers are filtered through diatomaceous earth and disinfected by cold filtration (0.45 μm filter) or pasteurisation before packaging.

🍷 Wine Production

Wine is produced from the fermentation of fruit juice, most commonly grapes. Grapes are crushed to form "must".

White Wine

White grapes are used. Skins are removed (pressing) before fermentation begins.

Red Wine

Red/black grapes are used. Skins remain during fermentation, providing colour and tannins.

Rosé Wine

Red grapes are used, but juice is only kept in contact with the skins briefly to develop a pink colour.

Secondary Fermentation

Takes 3–6 months in stainless steel vessels or oak barrels. Malolactic fermentation converts sharp malic acid to softer lactic acid.

🥖 Bread

Bread leavening involves the growth of Saccharomyces cerevisiae (baker's yeast) under aerobic conditions, maximising CO₂ production which causes the dough to rise. Sourdough breads use different microbial communities for distinct flavours. Spoilage can occur through Bacillus species that produce a condition called ropiness in bread.

🥛 Yogurt

Yogurt is made by fermenting milk with a mixture of Streptococcus salivarius ssp. thermophilus and Lactobacillus delbrueckii ssp. bulgaricus. The lactic acid produced causes milk protein (casein) to coagulate into a semi-solid curd. Additional probiotics like L. acidophilus, L. casei, and Bifidobacterium species are often co-cultured for health benefits.

🧀 Cheese

Cheese production begins with treating milk with lactic acid bacteria and rennin (an enzyme that partially hydrolyses casein, causing it to coagulate into curds). The liquid whey is separated from the curds, which are then aged (ripened). Different microorganisms active during early and late ripening stages create the vast diversity of cheese types and flavours we know today.

Unit 1 Food Safety

How Pathogens Cause Illness

Foodborne pathogens cause illness through two distinct mechanisms. Understanding the difference is critical for correct treatment and prevention.

🧫 Foodborne Intoxication

Bacteria grow in the food before it is eaten and produce toxins. When the food is consumed, it is the toxin — not the bacteria — that causes illness.

⚠️ Even if you kill the bacteria by reheating, the toxin can remain and still cause illness.

Examples: Clostridium botulinum, Staphylococcus aureus

🦠 Foodborne Infection

Bacteria enter the food, the contaminated food is ingested, and bacteria then grow within the intestines and produce toxins inside the body.

Examples: Campylobacter, Salmonella, E. coli, Listeria, Shigella, Vibrio

Mechanisms of Pathogenesis

  • Attachment: using pili or adhesin proteins to bind to the intestinal wall
  • Toxin production — Type 1: toxins that increase secretion of water and electrolytes (causing watery diarrhoea)
  • Toxin production — Type 2: toxins that directly kill host cells (causing bloody diarrhoea)
  • Alterations in host cells: injecting effector proteins that change cell behaviour (A/E lesions)
  • Cell invasion: actually invading and living inside intestinal cells (e.g. Salmonella, Shigella)

Unit 1 Food Safety

Prevention of Foodborne Illness

Most foodborne illnesses are preventable through five core principles of food safety, commonly summarised as the food safety fundamentals.

  1. Cook — Cook all meat, poultry, and eggs to at least 73°C (163°F). At this temperature, almost all bacteria, parasites, and viruses are destroyed. Note: spore-forming bacteria like Clostridium are resistant to cooking temperatures.
  2. Avoid Cross-Contamination — Keep raw and cooked foods completely separated. Use separate utensils, cutting boards, and surfaces for raw and cooked products. Constantly think about how microorganisms could transfer from raw to cooked foods.
  3. Chill Foods Rapidly — After cooking, chill food as quickly as possible. Cooking destroys most bacteria, but spore-formers (which survive cooking) can become very active and multiply rapidly if food cools slowly.
  4. Clean Everything — Wash fruits, vegetables, and all foods possible. Continuously clean work surfaces and equipment. Use only treated or tested water for food preparation.
  5. Personal Hygiene — Food handlers must wash hands regularly, wear hairnets and gloves, and must not handle food if they have open sores, boils, or feel unwell. Many outbreaks are directly traced to infected food workers.
Unit 1 Laboratory Methods

Laboratory Analysis of Food

Bacteriological analysis of food involves quantifying the total microbial load and identifying specific pathogens through culture methods and molecular techniques.

Standard Plate Count Procedure

Period 1 — Sample Preparation

  1. Weigh 10 g of the food sample and add to a sterile blending jar with 90 ml of sterile saline. Blend at high speed for 1–3 minutes to make a uniform slurry. This is the 10⁻¹ dilution.
  2. Prepare serial dilutions (10⁻², 10⁻³, 10⁻⁴, 10⁻⁵) by transferring 1.0 ml at each step into 9 ml of sterile saline. Mix thoroughly before each transfer.
  3. Transfer 0.1 ml from each dilution onto a separate nutrient agar plate using a glass spreader.
  4. Incubate all plates inverted for 2 days at 37°C.

Period 2 — Colony Counting

Count the number of colonies on each plate using a colony counter. Record counts. Only plates with 30–300 colonies are considered countable (within the valid range). Calculate the total viable count (TVC) using:

TVC (CFU/g) = Colony count × (1 / Volume plated) × Dilution factor

Molecular Methods — PCR

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) is used to detect pathogenic organisms in food products. Commercial kits are available for nucleic acid extraction, PCR detection, and differentiation of specific pathogens. PCR amplifies trace amounts of pathogen DNA to detectable levels, making it far more sensitive than traditional culture methods. It is widely used in modern food safety testing to prevent foodborne illness outbreaks before they occur.

🧬
Why PCR MattersTraditional culture methods can take 24–72 hours. PCR can deliver results in a few hours, allowing contaminated food to be identified and removed from supply chains much faster.
Unit 2 Milk Microbiology

Milk Composition

Milk is a complex biological fluid produced by mammary glands. Its composition is highly nutritious — making it ideal for both human consumption and microbial growth.

ConstituentMean PercentageKey Notes
Water87%Largest single component; solvent for all other constituents
Fat4%Present as fat globules; solvent for fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K
Protein (mostly casein)3.4%Casein = 80% of milk protein; essential for cheese making
Lactose4.8%Primary carbohydrate; substrate for fermentation in yogurt and cheese
Minerals0.8%Chlorides, phosphates, citrates of Na, Ca, Mg — influence coagulation

Milk Fat

About 98% of milk fat consists of triacylglycerols, along with smaller amounts of free fatty acids, phospholipids, sterols, and hydrocarbons. Milk fat also contains fat-soluble pigments such as carotene (which gives butter its yellow colour). When milk is left to stand, fat globules rise to form a cream layer — a process called creaming.

💡
Lipases — A Spoilage EnzymeAmong the enzymes naturally present in milk, lipases are particularly important because they cause rancidity (off-flavours from fat breakdown), especially in homogenised milk where the fat globule membrane has been disrupted. Phosphatase enzymes are also naturally present and their absence after pasteurisation is used as a test to confirm adequate heat treatment.
Unit 2 Milk Chemistry

Milk Proteins

Milk proteins are polymers of amino acids that perform structural and functional roles. They are the key components exploited in dairy product manufacturing.

Casein

Casein is the dominant protein group in milk, making up approximately 80% of total milk protein (bovine milk contains about 3.5% total protein). It can be separated from milk by acid precipitation or by adding rennin. In cheesemaking, most casein is recovered together with the milk fat as curds. Casein can also be recovered from skim milk as a commercial product.

Whey Proteins

Whey proteins are the group of proteins that remain in the liquid (whey) after casein coagulates during cheesemaking. They include several distinct proteins and also contain fragments of casein molecules. Whey protein is widely used as a nutritional supplement and food ingredient due to its high biological value.

Milk Salts & Vitamins

Salts

Mainly chlorides, phosphates, and citrates of sodium, calcium, and magnesium. Though less than 1% of milk, they strongly influence the rate of protein coagulation and other functional properties essential for cheese and yogurt making.

Vitamins

Fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) are associated with the fat fraction. Water-soluble (B complex, C) are in the water phase. Vitamins are unstable — processing (especially heat) reduces their content.

Unit 2 Microbial Growth in Milk

Milk as a Microbial Environment

In addition to being nutritious for humans, milk's rich composition makes it an ideal growth medium for microorganisms. Understanding how microbes enter and grow in milk is fundamental to dairy safety.

🌡️
Critical Temperature WindowFreshly drawn milk is approximately 38°C — ideal for rapid bacterial growth. If milk is not cooled and stored in the shade at 16°C ambient temperature, the milk temperature only falls to 28°C after 3 hours. At these temperatures, bacteria multiply very rapidly and milk sours quickly.

Sources of Microbial Contamination

  • The cow itself: bacteria from the udder, teat canal, and skin
  • Air: airborne organisms settle into open milk containers
  • Feedstuffs: silage and other feeds introduce environmental organisms
  • Milk handling equipment: improperly cleaned pipes, tanks, and milking machines
  • The milker: human skin flora and poor hygiene practices

Cooling as the Primary Control Measure

It is far more effective to prevent microbial contamination and control growth through immediate cooling than to try to control multiplication after it has begun. Cooling effects on milk temperature:

No Cooling (shade at 16°C)

Milk temperature falls from 38°C to only 28°C after 3 hours → rapid bacterial growth

Cooling with Running Water

Temperature reduced to 16°C after 1 hour → bacterial growth greatly retarded; enzyme activity slowed

Unit 2 Milk Safety

Milk Pasteurisation

Pasteurisation is the most important public health intervention in dairy — it destroys pathogenic organisms while preserving the nutritional quality and sensory properties of milk.

✅
Standard HTST PasteurisationThe most widely used method is High Temperature Short Time (HTST) — heating milk to 72°C for 15 seconds, followed by rapid cooling to below 10°C. This is carried out continuously using a plate heat-exchanger and a holding section.

Effects of Pasteurisation on Milk

✅ What Pasteurisation Does

  • Kills all pathogenic microorganisms
  • Kills many fermentative spoilage organisms
  • Extends storage stability to 2–3 days under refrigeration
  • Slightly reduces the cream layer (fat globule clustering reduced)

⚠️ What Pasteurisation Does NOT Do

  • Does not reduce fat content of milk
  • Does not significantly harm major nutrients
  • Does not kill spore-forming bacteria
  • Does not eliminate all microorganisms — surviving putrefactive organisms cause deterioration after 2–3 days
💊
Vitamin ImpactPasteurisation causes some loss of vitamin C and B-group vitamins, but this loss is considered insignificant nutritionally, since milk is not a primary source of these vitamins in most diets.

Uses of Pasteurisation in Dairy

  • Treatment of liquid milk prior to retail sale
  • Reduction of microbial counts in milk destined for cheesemaking
  • Treatment of cream prior to butter making in some factories
Unit 2 Milk Safety

Sterilisation & UHT Treatment

When pasteurisation is not enough — sterilisation applies severe heat to achieve near-complete destruction of all microorganisms, producing a shelf-stable product.

🔥 Conventional Sterilisation

Milk is subjected to temperatures above 100°C for 15–40 minutes. This achieves commercial sterility — the product is free from organisms capable of growth under normal storage conditions. Has a significantly longer shelf life than pasteurised milk but does alter taste due to prolonged heat exposure.

⚡ Ultra-High Temperature (UHT)

Milk is heated under pressure to approximately 140°C for just 4 seconds. The product is virtually sterile yet retains more of the organoleptic (taste, colour, nutritional) properties of fresh milk compared to conventionally sterilised milk. UHT milk can be stored at room temperature for months when packaged aseptically.

Unit 2 Quality Testing

Microbial Tests for Raw & Pasteurised Milk

Three standard tests are used to assess the microbiological quality and safety of milk in commercial settings.

Test 1: Total Bacteria Count

Measures the total number of bacteria in a milk sample that can grow and form countable colonies on Standard Methods Agar after incubation at 32°C for 48 hours. A high count indicates poor hygiene during milking, inadequate cooling, or contamination during processing. Results are expressed as Colony Forming Units per millilitre (CFU/ml).

Test 2: Coliform Count

Counts colonies that grow on Violet Red Bile Agar (VRBA) at 32°C for 24 hours. Coliforms are indicator organisms — their presence indicates faecal or environmental contamination. A high coliform count signals a breakdown in hygiene. If coliforms are detected in pasteurised milk, it indicates post-pasteurisation contamination.

Test 3: Somatic Cell Count (SCC)

Somatic cells are blood cells (white blood cells) that occur naturally in milk as part of the immune response. A high somatic cell count indicates mastitis — an infection of the mammary gland. The SCC can be determined by direct microscopic examination or by automated electronic cell counters. High SCC affects milk quality and is used as a regulatory measure for farm milk quality.

Unit 2 Food Safety

Antibiotics in Milk

The use of antibiotics to treat mastitis in dairy cows creates a significant food safety concern — antibiotic residues in milk.

💊
Why This MattersCows treated for mastitis with antibiotics may have antibiotic residues in their milk during and after treatment. Milk containing these residues must not enter the food supply. Antibiotic residues in milk can: interfere with dairy starter cultures (disrupting yogurt and cheese production); cause allergic reactions in sensitive individuals; and contribute to antimicrobial resistance.
  • Milk from antibiotic-treated cows must be discarded or collected separately and not used for human consumption
  • The legal standard (FDA) requires that milk contain no detectable antibiotics when tested with approved methods
  • Farms with positive antibiotic test results face regulatory action
Unit 2 Dairy Science

Factors Affecting Milk Composition

Milk composition is not constant — it varies considerably based on a complex interplay of genetic, physiological, and environmental factors.

1. Genetics & Breed

Jersey and Guernsey cattle produce milk with higher fat and protein content than Shorthorn or Friesian breeds. Zebu cows can produce milk with up to 7% fat. Selective breeding can improve milk quality since fat, protein, and lactose levels are all genetically determined.

2. Milking Interval

Fat content varies between morning and evening milkings due to unequal intervals between milkings. If cows were milked at exactly 12-hour intervals, the fat variation would be negligible. Solids-not-fat (SNF) content is relatively unaffected by milking intervals.

3. Stage of Lactation

SNF is highest in the first 2–3 weeks post-calving, then decreases slightly. Fat content is initially high after calving, falls for 10–12 weeks, then rises again toward the end of lactation. Fat, protein, and lactose all vary throughout the lactation cycle.

4. Age of the Cow

As cows age, fat content decreases by approximately 0.02 percentage units per lactation. The decline in solids-not-fat content is proportionally greater.

5. Feeding Regime

Underfeeding reduces both fat and SNF content. Fat content and composition are more strongly influenced by roughage (fibre) intake. SNF is sensitive to energy intake but not significantly affected by protein deficiency unless deficiency is severe.

6. Disease (especially Mastitis)

Mastitis significantly reduces both fat and SNF content of milk. It increases somatic cell count and can alter protein composition. Milk from mastitic quarters is of lower quality and may contain pathogens.

7. Completeness of Milking

The first milk drawn (foremilk) is significantly lower in fat compared to the last milk drawn (strippings/hindmilk), which is always high in fat. It is therefore essential to thoroughly mix all milk removed from the udder before taking any sample for compositional analysis. Any fat remaining in the udder at the end of a milking is recovered during subsequent milkings, so there is no net fat loss.
