Section A — Multiple Choice Questions

50 Questions · 1 Mark Each
Q1What is the primary purpose of bacteriological water analysis?
ATo detect chemical pollutants in water
BTo estimate the number and type of bacteria present in water
CTo measure the pH and temperature of water
DTo identify heavy metals in water samples
Correct: B. Bacteriological water analysis estimates the numbers (concentration) and type of bacteria present — one aspect of overall water quality assessment.
Q2Which organism is considered the gold standard indicator of faecal contamination in water?
AEnterobacter aerogenes
BClostridium perfringens
CEscherichia coli
DPseudomonas aeruginosa
Correct: C. E. coli is the only true faecal coliform, found exclusively in the intestinal tract of warm-blooded animals. Its presence in water is definitive proof of recent faecal contamination.
Q3The coliform group is characterised by which of the following features?
AGram-positive, spore-forming, ferment glucose to acid only
BGram-negative, non-spore-forming, ferment lactose to acid and gas
CGram-negative, spore-forming, aerobic only
DGram-positive, non-spore-forming, ferment lactose to gas only
Correct: B. Coliforms are defined as Gram-negative, non-spore-forming, aerobic to facultatively anaerobic rods that ferment lactose to produce both acid AND gas.
Q4At what temperature do thermotolerant (faecal) coliforms grow and ferment lactose?
A35–37°C
B37–40°C
C44–44.5°C
D50–55°C
Correct: C. Thermotolerant (faecal) coliforms are defined by their ability to grow and ferment lactose at 44–44.5°C, which distinguishes them from environmental coliforms that cannot tolerate this elevated temperature.
Q5What percentage of thermotolerant coliforms isolated from water are typically E. coli?
AMore than 50%
BMore than 75%
CMore than 95%
DExactly 100%
Correct: C. Usually more than 95% of thermotolerant coliforms isolated from water are E. coli, making their presence near-definitive proof of faecal contamination.
Q6Which test uses EMB (Eosin Methylene Blue) agar in standard water analysis?
APresumptive Test
BConfirmed Test
CCompleted Test
DMPN Test
Correct: B. EMB agar is used in the Confirmed Test. It is inoculated from a positive presumptive tube to confirm that gas production was due to coliforms, not other organisms.
Q7On EMB agar, E. coli colonies are characterised by:
ALarge pinkish colonies
BColourless colonies on a red background
CSmall colonies with a green metallic sheen
DYellow colonies with black centres
Correct: C. E. coli produces small colonies with a distinctive green metallic sheen on EMB agar. E. aerogenes, by contrast, forms large pinkish colonies.
Q8If only Enterobacter aerogenes appears on an EMB plate during the Confirmed Test, the result is interpreted as:
APositive — faecal contamination confirmed
BNegative — no faecal contamination indicated
CInconclusive — repeat the test
DPositive — E. aerogenes is always faecal
Correct: B. E. aerogenes is widely distributed in nature outside the intestinal tract (soil, plants), so its presence alone does NOT indicate faecal contamination. The test is negative.
Q9The Completed Test confirms coliforms when the organism produces gas in lactose broth AND is:
AGram-positive, spore-forming rod
BGram-negative, non-spore-forming rod
CGram-negative coccus in chains
DGram-positive, non-spore-forming coccus
Correct: B. The completed test is positive when the organism is a Gram-negative, non-spore-forming rod AND produces gas in the lactose tube — definitively confirming coliforms.
Q10What does MPN stand for in water microbiology?
AMinimum Pathogen Number
BMost Probable Number
CMaximum Permitted Number
DMicrobial Population Number
Correct: B. MPN — Most Probable Number — is a statistical estimate of the number of viable coliforms per 100 ml of water, derived from the pattern of positive tubes in a multiple tube test.
Q11Which indicator organism is most useful for detecting contamination from distant or old sources due to its environmental persistence?
ATotal coliforms
BThermotolerant coliforms
CFaecal streptococci
DPseudomonas aeruginosa
Correct: C. Faecal streptococci persist much longer in the environment than coliforms and are highly resistant to drying. They may be detected when contamination is old or the source is far from the sampling point.
Q12Sulphite-reducing Clostridia are particularly useful for examining marine sediments to detect sewage outfall zones. What is the approximate detection range?
AUp to 1 km from the outfall
BUp to 5 km from the outfall
CMore than 10 km from the outfall
DOnly at the outfall point itself
Correct: C. Sulphite-reducing Clostridia spores are extremely resistant and can be used to detect the zone affected by sewage outfall over a range greater than 10 km.
Q13In the membrane filtration method, what pore size is the cellulose acetate membrane?
A0.22 µm
B0.45 µm
C1.0 µm
D2.0 µm
Correct: B. In membrane filtration, water is vacuum-filtered through a cellulose acetate membrane with uniform pore diameter of 0.45 µm — small enough to retain bacteria on the surface.
Q14What is the optimum number of colonies per membrane for reliable counting in membrane filtration?
A5–15 colonies
B20–80 colonies
C100–200 colonies
D300–500 colonies
Correct: B. The optimum counting range for membrane filtration is 20–80 colonies per membrane — enough for statistical reliability but not so many that colonies overlap.
Q15Which method is NOT suitable for turbid water containing high levels of suspended material?
AMultiple tube fermentation technique
BMembrane filtration method
CPour plate method
DATP testing
Correct: B. Membrane filtration is unsuitable for turbid water because sediment, sludge, and suspended material clog the membrane before an adequate volume of water can pass through.
Q16In ATP testing, what enzyme is used to detect ATP by producing bioluminescence?
AAmylase
BLuciferase
CProtease
DOxidase
Correct: B. Firefly luciferase catalyses the reaction between ATP and luciferin to produce light. The amount of light is directly proportional to the amount of ATP (and therefore living microorganisms) in the sample.
Q17What is the target colony range for a valid plate count?
A10–50 colonies
B30–300 colonies
C100–500 colonies
D500–1000 colonies
Correct: B. The plate count method requires 30–300 colonies. Fewer than 30 is statistically unreliable; more than 300 leads to overlapping colonies and inaccurate counts.
Q18MacConkey agar inhibits Gram-positive bacteria primarily through which component?
ANeutral red dye and lactose
BBile salts and crystal violet dye
CEosin and methylene blue
DSodium azide and rosolic acid
Correct: B. MacConkey agar contains bile salts (primary inhibitor of Gram-positives) and crystal violet dye (additional inhibitor of certain Gram-positive bacteria). Neutral red stains lactose fermenters but does not inhibit growth.
Q19TYEA medium is classified as:
AHighly selective for faecal coliforms
BSelective for Gram-positive organisms
CNon-selective, used for general colony count
DSelective for anaerobic organisms only
Correct: C. TYEA is a non-selective medium used to determine the general level of contamination (colony count). It contains tryptone, yeast extract, common salt, and L-arabinose, and is incubated at both 22°C and 36°C.
Q20Which of the following is a waterborne protozoan pathogen highly resistant to chlorination?
ASalmonella typhi
BRotavirus
CCryptosporidia
DShigella
Correct: C. Cryptosporidia (a protozoan) is highly resistant to chlorination at standard water treatment doses. This makes it a particularly dangerous waterborne pathogen in treated municipal water supplies.
Q21Which waterborne virus causes paralysis?
ARotavirus
BHepatitis A
CPoliovirus
DNorwalk virus
Correct: C. Poliovirus is the waterborne enteric virus responsible for poliomyelitis, which can cause permanent paralysis. It is transmitted through the faecal-oral route via contaminated water.
Q22Faecal streptococci are detected in the laboratory by:
AProduction of a green metallic sheen on EMB agar
BGas production at 37°C in lactose broth
CReduction of a tetrazolium dye or hydrolysis of aesculin
DBioluminescence using luciferase
Correct: C. Faecal streptococci are grown on sodium azide-containing media at 37–44°C and are detected by reduction of a tetrazolium-containing dye or by hydrolysis of aesculin.
Q23In the multiple tube method, what is the purpose of the inverted Durham tube?
ATo measure the volume of water sample added
BTo trap gas produced during fermentation
CTo add additional growth medium
DTo prevent contamination of the broth
Correct: B. The Durham tube is a small inverted test tube placed inside the broth tube. It traps any gas produced during lactose fermentation, providing a visible indicator of positive coliform activity.
Q24Which agar was originally developed for isolation of Salmonella typhi but is now commonly used in water analysis?
AMacConkey agar
BmFC medium
CEndo agar
DTYEA medium
Correct: C. Endo agar (mEndo agar) was originally developed for isolating Salmonella typhi but is now widely used in membrane filtration for total coliform detection in water.
Q25What does VRBA stand for?
AViolet Red Bile Agar
BVariable Red Bacterial Assay
CViable Red Blood Agar
DViolet Rose Bile Agar
Correct: A. VRBA stands for Violet Red Bile Agar. It contains bile salts that promote the growth of Gram-negative bacteria (including coliforms) and inhibit Gram-positive organisms. Used in the direct plate count method.
Q26The pour plate method is primarily used for bacteria that:
AGrow vigorously on the agar surface in air
BGrow poorly in air (anaerobic/microaerophilic organisms)
CRequire high salt concentrations to grow
DOnly grow at temperatures above 50°C
Correct: B. The pour plate method mixes samples in molten agar which is sealed in bottles. This creates a semi-anaerobic environment within the agar body, ideal for organisms that grow poorly when exposed to air.
Q27Total Viable Count (TVC) is expressed in which units?
AMPN per 100 ml
Bcfu per ml
Cmg per litre
Dcells per µm²
Correct: B. TVC (Total Viable Count) is expressed as colony forming units per millilitre (cfu/ml). It is calculated by multiplying the counted colonies by the dilution factor used.
Q28Which component of mFC medium inhibits non-enteric bacteria?
AAniline blue
BRosolic acid
CBile salts
DCrystal violet
Correct: C. In mFC medium, bile salts inhibit non-enteric bacteria. Rosolic acid inhibits bacterial growth generally (except faecal coliforms), and aniline blue is the pH indicator showing lactose fermentation.
Q29In the Presumptive Test, nine tubes of double-strength lactose broth are inoculated with water in what volumes?
A100 ml, 10 ml, and 1 ml
B10 ml, 1.0 ml, and 0.1 ml
C50 ml, 5 ml, and 0.5 ml
D1 ml, 0.1 ml, and 0.01 ml
Correct: B. In the Presumptive Test, three tubes receive 10 ml, three receive 1.0 ml, and three receive 0.1 ml of the water sample, all into double-strength lactose broth, then incubated at 37°C for 24 hours.
Q30Which helminth is transmitted when its larvae penetrate the skin during water contact?
ARound worm (Ascaris)
BTape worm
CHook worm
DWhip worm
Correct: C. Hook worm larvae actively penetrate human skin during contact with contaminated water or soil. Round worm, tape worm, and whip worm are transmitted primarily through ingestion of contaminated food or water.
Q31The multiple fermentation tube technique gives results with which level of precision?
AHigh precision direct colony count
BLow precision indirect statistical approximation
CExact count to the nearest bacterium
DMedium precision by automated counting
Correct: B. The multiple tube technique gives low precision results obtained indirectly by statistical approximation (MPN tables). In contrast, membrane filtration gives high precision results from direct colony counts.
Q32Faecal streptococci belong to which Lancefield antigen group?
AGroup A
BGroup B
CGroup D
DGroup G
Correct: C. Faecal streptococci (S. faecalis, S. faecium, S. bovis) possess Lancefield Group D antigens. They also grow in the presence of bile salts, sodium azide, and at 45°C.
Q33Which waterborne disease is transmitted by the Anopheles mosquito that breeds in water?
ASchistosomiasis
BCholera
CMalaria
DTyphoid
Correct: C. Malaria is transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes that breed in stagnant water. It is classified as a water-related disease. Cholera and typhoid are direct waterborne bacterial diseases.
Q34Which statement about indicator organisms is CORRECT?
AIndicator organisms are always pathogenic
BInfected individuals excrete far more indicator organisms than actual pathogens
CIndicator organisms are difficult to detect in small samples
DThe presence of one pathogen confirms absence of all others
Correct: B. Even when a person is infected with a pathogen, they excrete many millions of times more indicator organisms than pathogens. This abundance makes indicators easy to detect even when pathogen numbers are too low to find.
Q35In the Presumptive Test, incubation is carried out at what temperature?
A25°C
B37°C
C44.5°C
D55°C
Correct: B. The Presumptive Test uses incubation at 37°C — the standard temperature for coliforms. Thermotolerant coliforms require the higher temperature of 44–44.5°C to distinguish them from environmental coliforms.
Q36Which cyanobacterium is classified as an algal waterborne pathogen?
ACryptosporidia
BMicrocystis
CGiardia
DAmoeba
Correct: B. Microcystis is a cyanobacterium (blue-green alga) that produces potent toxins in water. Dinoflagellates are another algal waterborne pathogen. Cryptosporidia and Giardia are protozoa; amoeba is also protozoan.
Q37Membrane filtration results are reported as:
AMPN per 100 ml
BThermotolerant coliforms [Number] cfu per 100 ml
CTotal viable count in cfu/g
DOptical density units per litre
Correct: B. Membrane filtration results for thermotolerant coliforms are expressed as "thermotolerant coliforms [Number] cfu per 100 ml." Each colony represents one colony-forming unit from bacteria retained on the membrane.
Q38What does a high total coliform count combined with a zero thermotolerant coliform count most likely indicate?
ADefinitive faecal contamination
BEntry of soil or organic matter — not necessarily faecal
CViral contamination of the water
DThe presence of Clostridium perfringens
Correct: B. A high total coliform count with zero thermotolerant coliforms does not necessarily indicate faecal contamination. It may be caused by entry of soil or organic matter, or conditions that favour non-faecal coliform growth.
Q39Which of these diseases is caused by waterborne bacteria that produce an enteric fever?
AMalaria
BTyphoid fever
CDengue fever
DYellow fever
Correct: B. Typhoid fever (caused by Salmonella typhi) is a waterborne bacterial disease classified as an enteric fever. Paratyphoid is also an enteric fever caused by Salmonella paratyphi. Malaria, dengue, and yellow fever are vector-borne.
Q40In the plate count method, incubation at 22°C is used to detect which type of organisms?
AFaecal/gut organisms
BThermophilic organisms
CEnvironmental/psychrotrophic organisms
DSpore-forming anaerobes
Correct: C. In the plate count method, 22°C incubation detects environmental (psychrotrophic) organisms, while 37°C incubation targets gut/faecal organisms. Running both temperatures gives a complete picture of contamination.
Q41The Completed Test uses organisms from the Confirmed Test to inoculate:
AA lactose broth tube only
BA nutrient agar slant only
CBoth a nutrient agar slant and a lactose broth tube
DAn EMB agar plate and a MacConkey plate
Correct: C. In the Completed Test, organisms from the EMB plate are used to inoculate BOTH a nutrient agar slant (for Gram staining) and a lactose broth tube (for gas production). Both results together confirm coliforms.
Q42Which component of mFC medium serves as a pH indicator for lactose fermentation by faecal coliforms?
ARosolic acid
BBile salts
CAniline blue
DCrystal violet
Correct: C. Aniline blue in mFC medium is the pH indicator. When faecal coliforms ferment lactose to acid, the pH drops and aniline blue changes colour, visually identifying faecal coliform colonies.
Q43The most common multiple fermentation tube procedure uses how many aliquots from each of three dilutions?
AThree aliquots
BFive aliquots
CTen aliquots
DOne aliquot
Correct: B. The standard procedure uses five aliquots from each of three consecutive 10-fold dilutions (e.g. 1:1, 1:10, 1:100), giving 15 tubes total. The pattern of positives at each dilution is used to determine the MPN.
Q44Rosolic acid in mFC medium functions to:
AProvide a carbon source for coliform growth
BInhibit general bacterial growth except faecal coliforms
CIndicate acid production from lactose fermentation
DEnhance growth of Gram-positive organisms
Correct: B. Rosolic acid in mFC medium inhibits general bacterial growth, allowing mainly faecal coliforms to grow. Bile salts specifically target non-enteric bacteria, while aniline blue serves as the pH indicator.
Q45Which waterborne pathogen causes liver damage with flu-like symptoms?
ARotavirus
BHepatitis A and E viruses
CE. coli O157:H7
DCampylobacter
Correct: B. Hepatitis A and E are waterborne viruses that cause flu-like illness with significant liver damage. Rotavirus and Norwalk virus cause diarrhoea/dysentery. E. coli and Campylobacter cause bacterial gastroenteritis.
Q46Which of the following correctly describes the E. coli colony appearance on MacConkey agar?
AColourless, non-lactose fermenting colonies
BPink/red colonies due to lactose fermentation detected by neutral red dye
CBlack colonies on pale background
DGreen metallic sheen colonies
Correct: B. On MacConkey agar, E. coli (a lactose fermenter) produces pink/red colonies because the acid produced from lactose fermentation causes the neutral red dye indicator to change colour. Green metallic sheen is specific to EMB agar.
Q47In membrane filtration, what is the consequence of testing two or more sample volumes when water quality is unknown?
AResults are averaged and divided
BColony counts from multiple membranes are added together for total count
CThe lowest colony count is taken as the result
DThe highest colony count is taken as the result
Correct: B. If a single membrane cannot accommodate the required volume, the sample is filtered through two or more membranes and the colony counts from all membranes are added together to give the total count.
Q48Total coliforms are grown in the laboratory at which temperature?
A22–25°C
B35–37°C
C44–44.5°C
D55–60°C
Correct: B. Total coliforms are grown at 35–37°C in or on a lactose-containing medium and provisionally identified by acid and gas production from lactose fermentation.
Q49Which statement best explains why E. coli, not E. aerogenes, is used as a faecal indicator?
AE. coli produces gas while E. aerogenes does not
BE. coli is found only in faeces of warm-blooded animals; E. aerogenes is widespread in the environment
CE. coli is easier to culture than E. aerogenes
DE. aerogenes is pathogenic while E. coli is not
Correct: B. E. coli is the only true faecal coliform — found exclusively in intestinal tracts of warm-blooded animals. E. aerogenes is distributed widely in nature (soil, plants) outside the gut, so its presence in water does not indicate faecal contamination.
Q50Which of the following is an advantage of the multiple tube technique over membrane filtration?
AFaster turnaround time
BApplicable to all types of water including turbid samples
CLower cost of consumables
DBoth B and C
Correct: D. The multiple tube technique is applicable to ALL water types (including turbid water that blocks membrane filters) AND uses consumables that are widely available at lower cost in most countries. Membrane filtration is faster and more precise but limited to clear water and has higher consumable costs.

SA1Define bacteriological water analysis and state ONE reason why it is routinely performed.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Bacteriological water analysis is the method of examining water to estimate the number (concentration) and type of bacteria present (1 mark). It is routinely performed to ensure a safe supply of water for drinking, bathing, swimming, and other domestic and industrial uses (1 mark).
SA2List FOUR categories of waterborne microbial pathogens.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Any four of: Bacteria, Viruses, Protozoa, Helminths, Spirochetes, Rickettsia, Algae. (½ mark each, max 2 marks)
SA3State TWO reasons why it is impractical to test water for every individual pathogen.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Any two of: (i) Individual pathogen numbers may be too low to detect in a reasonably-sized water sample; (ii) Isolation and detection of some pathogens can take days, weeks, or months; (iii) Numerous pathogens exist and testing each is impossible; (iv) Absence of one pathogen does not rule out others. (1 mark each)
SA4Define an indicator organism and name TWO examples used in water bacteriology.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
An indicator organism is a bacterium commonly found in the human or animal gut which, if detected in water, suggests the presence of sewage/faecal contamination (1 mark). Examples: E. coli, non-specific coliforms, Pseudomonas aeruginosa (any two — ½ mark each).
SA5Distinguish between total coliforms and thermotolerant coliforms in terms of their growth temperature and significance.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Total coliforms grow at 35–37°C and may not indicate faecal contamination (they include environmental organisms) (1 mark). Thermotolerant coliforms grow at 44–44.5°C and nearly always indicate faecal contamination; over 95% are E. coli (1 mark).
SA6What is the purpose of the Presumptive Test and what constitutes a positive result?
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
The Presumptive Test screens for possible coliform presence by detecting lactose fermentation and gas production in lactose broth tubes (1 mark). A positive result is gas production in ANY one of the inoculated tubes, which is presumptive evidence of coliforms — indicating the water may be unsafe (1 mark).
SA7Explain why the Confirmed Test is necessary after a positive Presumptive Test.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Gas production in the Presumptive Test is not specific to coliforms — other non-coliform bacteria may also produce gas from lactose (1 mark). The Confirmed Test (using EMB agar) is therefore necessary to confirm that gas production was specifically caused by coliforms, and to distinguish E. coli (faecal indicator) from E. aerogenes (non-faecal) (1 mark).
SA8State the distinguishing colony characteristics of E. coli and E. aerogenes on EMB agar.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
E. coli produces small colonies with a distinctive green metallic sheen on EMB agar (1 mark). E. aerogenes forms large, pinkish colonies without metallic sheen (1 mark). Only the presence of E. coli (with or without E. aerogenes) constitutes a positive confirmed test.
SA9What is MPN? How is it determined in the multiple tube technique?
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
MPN (Most Probable Number) is a statistical estimate of the number of viable coliforms per 100 ml of water (1 mark). It is determined by comparing the pattern of positive tubes (number of gas-producing tubes at each dilution) to statistical MPN tables (1 mark).
SA10Describe the principle of the membrane filtration method for detecting coliforms.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
A measured volume of water is vacuum-filtered through a cellulose acetate membrane (0.45 µm pore size) that retains bacteria on its surface (1 mark). The membrane is placed on a selective medium and incubated; if coliforms are present, characteristic colonies form and are counted directly — giving a precise cfu per 100 ml result (1 mark).
SA11State TWO advantages of membrane filtration over the multiple tube technique.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Any two of: (i) Faster — quantitative results in about 18 hours vs 48 hours; (ii) Direct colony count giving higher precision vs statistical approximation; (iii) Less labour-intensive; (iv) Requires less culture medium and glassware; (v) Readily adapted for field use. (1 mark each)
SA12Why is membrane filtration unsuitable for highly turbid water samples?
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
High levels of suspended material (sludge, sediment, particulates) physically block the 0.45 µm membrane pores (1 mark), preventing an adequate volume of water from passing through before the membrane becomes clogged — making the method unworkable for turbid samples (1 mark).
SA13Explain the principle behind ATP testing for detecting microorganisms in water.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is a molecule found only in and around living cells, so its detection gives a direct measure of biological concentration (1 mark). ATP is quantified by reacting it with the enzyme firefly luciferase to produce bioluminescence; the amount of light produced (measured by a luminometer) is directly proportional to the amount of ATP (and therefore living microorganisms) present (1 mark).
SA14State the acceptable colony range for the plate count method and explain why very low or very high counts are problematic.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
The target range is 30–300 colonies per plate (½ mark). Fewer than 30 colonies makes the interpretation statistically unreliable (½ mark); more than 300 colonies leads to overlapping colonies, making accurate counting impossible (½ mark). Serial dilutions are made to ensure the count falls in this optimal range (½ mark).
SA15What is the purpose of the pour plate method and what organism count does it measure?
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
The pour plate method is used for bacteria that grow poorly in air (anaerobic/microaerophilic organisms). Samples are mixed in molten agar, poured into sealed bottles, and incubated, allowing colonies to grow throughout the agar body (1 mark). It measures the Total Viable Count (TVC), expressed in cfu/ml, calculated as colony count × dilution factor (1 mark).
SA16Name the components of MacConkey agar and the role of each.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Bile salts — inhibit most Gram-positive bacteria; Crystal violet dye — additional inhibition of certain Gram-positive bacteria; Neutral red dye — stains lactose-fermenting colonies pink/red; Lactose — fermented by target organisms; Peptone — nitrogen/carbon source for growth. (½ mark per component with role, max 2 marks)
SA17What are faecal streptococci and why are they useful indicators when thermotolerant coliforms are absent?
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Faecal streptococci (e.g. S. faecalis, S. faecium, S. bovis) are Gram-positive, chain-forming cocci with Lancefield Group D antigens, found in the gut of warm-blooded animals (1 mark). They persist much longer in the environment and are more resistant to drying than coliforms, so they may be detected even when contamination is old or geographically distant — useful when coliforms have already died off (1 mark).
SA18State TWO waterborne bacterial diseases that cause enteric fever.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Typhoid fever (caused by Salmonella typhi) and Paratyphoid fever (caused by Salmonella paratyphi). (1 mark each)
SA19Describe Sulphite-reducing Clostridia and state TWO situations where they are most useful as indicators.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
They are Gram-positive, anaerobic, spore-forming rods whose spores survive much longer than other indicators and are highly resistant to chlorination (1 mark). They are most useful for: (i) water supplies sampled only occasionally with intermittent contamination (e.g. shallow wells/springs), and (ii) examining marine sediments to detect zones affected by sewage outfall up to 10+ km away (1 mark).
SA20What is a Colony Forming Unit (cfu) and how is it used in membrane filtration results?
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
A colony forming unit (cfu) is one bacterium, clump of bacteria, or particle with bacteria attached that gives rise to a single visible colony on culture medium (1 mark). In membrane filtration, results are expressed as the number of thermotolerant coliforms [cfu] per 100 ml — each colony on the membrane counts as one cfu (1 mark).
SA21Give TWO specific reasons why E. coli (not E. aerogenes) is used as an indicator of faecal contamination.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
(i) E. coli is found exclusively in the intestinal tract of warm-blooded animals and is not normally found in fresh water or soil — its presence in water therefore definitively indicates faecal contamination (1 mark). (ii) E. aerogenes is widely distributed in nature outside the intestinal tract (soil, plants), so its presence in water cannot be attributed to faecal contamination (1 mark).
SA22Why does the multiple fermentation tube technique use five aliquots from each of three dilutions?
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Using five aliquots (replicates) at each dilution level increases the sensitivity and statistical reliability of the MPN estimate (1 mark). Three consecutive 10-fold dilutions ensure that at least one dilution falls within the range where growth can be detected and the MPN accurately estimated — covering a wide range of possible bacterial concentrations (1 mark).
SA23State the role of aniline blue in mFC medium and the role of TYEA medium in water analysis.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Aniline blue in mFC medium is a pH indicator — it changes colour when faecal coliforms ferment lactose to acid, allowing visual identification of faecal coliform colonies (1 mark). TYEA is a non-selective medium used to determine the general level of contamination (colony count); it is cultivated at both 22°C and 36°C to capture environmental and gut organisms respectively (1 mark).
SA24Name THREE waterborne protozoan pathogens and state what feature of Cryptosporidia makes it particularly dangerous in treated water.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Three waterborne protozoa: Giardia, Cryptosporidia, Amoeba (1 mark for all three, or ½ for any two). Cryptosporidia is highly resistant to chlorination at standard water treatment doses, meaning it can survive and remain infectious even in chlorinated municipal water supplies (1 mark).
SA25Explain why all water microbiology methods rely on statistical principles.
2 marks
✅ Model Answer
Analysis is always performed on a very small sample volume taken from a very large volume of water (1 mark). Because the sample is only a tiny fraction of the total, results cannot be exact — they are estimates of the bacterial density in the whole water body. Statistical tools (e.g. MPN tables, serial dilution calculations) are used to extrapolate from the sample to the whole population (1 mark).

📋 Marking Scheme — 10 Marks
2Principle: Serial dilutions of water sample processed across multiple tubes. Individual portions inoculate culture medium, incubated at standard temperature. Coliforms indicated by turbidity, pH change, and/or gas production. Statistical basis — small sample from large volume; results rely on statistical tables.
2Standard procedure: Five aliquots from each of three consecutive 10-fold dilutions (e.g. undiluted, 1:10, 1:100) — 15 tubes total. 2MPN calculation: Count positive tubes at each of three dilutions. Compare pattern of positives (e.g. 5-3-1) to MPN statistical tables. Tabulated value reported as MPN per 100 ml. More tubes used = more sensitive and reliable estimate.
2Comparison: Multiple tube is more sensitive but gives low-precision indirect statistical results; slower (48 hrs for confirmed positive); applicable to all water types. Membrane filtration is faster (18 hrs), gives high-precision direct colony counts (cfu), but less sensitive and unsuitable for turbid water.
